# Chapter 4 — Reviews: Exam Traps

## Trap 1: Confusing Walkthrough and Inspection

**Trap:** Walkthroughs are led by the author and less formal. Inspections are led by a moderator and highly formal.

**Example question:**
> In which review type does the AUTHOR typically lead the session?
>
> A. Inspection
> B. Technical Review
> C. Walkthrough
> D. Informal Review

**Wrong answer:** A — "Inspection" — inspections are led by a trained moderator, not the author.

**Why it's wrong:** In inspections, the author does not lead — a trained moderator facilitates. The author only answers questions and listens.

**Correct answer:** C — Walkthrough.

**Defense strategy:** Walkthrough = author presents and explains. Inspection = moderator leads, author does not defend.

---

## Trap 2: Thinking the Author Should Defend in Formal Reviews

**Trap:** In formal reviews (inspections), the author does NOT defend the work product.

**Example question:**
> During an inspection, the author of the document being reviewed should:
>
> A. Defend the document against all reviewer criticisms
> B. Explain the document and justify design decisions
> C. Listen to comments, answer factual questions, and not defend
> D. Lead the meeting and guide reviewers through the document

**Wrong answer:** A or B — the author defending or justifying.

**Why it's wrong:** In inspections, the author listens to comments and answers factual questions only. Defending is counterproductive.

**Correct answer:** C — Listen, answer factual questions, and not defend.

**Defense strategy:** In formal reviews, the focus is on finding defects, not debating them. The author fixes defects during rework.

---

## Trap 3: Confusing Manager and Moderator Roles

**Trap:** The manager decides whether to review; the moderator plans and leads the review.

**Example question:**
> Who is responsible for LEADING the review meeting and keeping it focused?
>
> A. The Manager
> B. The Author
> C. The Moderator
> D. The Scribe

**Wrong answer:** A — "The Manager" — the manager decides resources but does not lead the meeting.

**Why it's wrong:** The manager allocates resources and approves the review. The moderator facilitates the meeting.

**Correct answer:** C — The Moderator.

**Defense strategy:** Manager = decision to review + resources. Moderator = planning + facilitating. Author = creator + fixes defects. Scribe = records issues.

---

## Trap 4: Thinking Reviews Are for Solving Problems

**Trap:** Review meetings identify defects; solutions are found during rework, not during the meeting.

**Example question:**
> During a technical review meeting, a reviewer identifies a complex architectural issue. The team starts discussing how to fix it. What should the moderator do?
>
> A. Allow the discussion to continue since it's productive
> B. Record the issue and schedule a separate problem-solving session
> C. Ask the author to fix it immediately during the meeting
> D. Dismiss the issue as out of scope

**Wrong answer:** A — allowing the meeting to drift into problem-solving.

**Why it's wrong:** Review meetings identify WHAT is wrong, not HOW to fix it. Problem-solving during the meeting derails the review and wastes time.

**Correct answer:** B — Record the issue and schedule separate problem-solving.

**Defense strategy:** "Focus on defects, not solutions" is a key review principle. Solutions come during rework.

---

## Trap 5: Forgetting Entry and Exit Criteria for Formal Reviews

**Trap:** Formal reviews (inspections, technical reviews) require entry and exit criteria.

**Example question:**
> Which review type MOST typically requires entry criteria (e.g., document completeness, standards compliance) before the review can begin?
>
> A. Informal Review
> B. Walkthrough
> C. Technical Review
> D. Inspection

**Wrong answer:** C — while technical reviews are formal, inspections are the MOST formal.

**Why it's wrong:** Inspections are the most formal and have the strictest entry/exit criteria.

**Correct answer:** D — Inspection.

**Defense strategy:** Formality increases: Informal < Walkthrough < Technical Review < Inspection. Entry/exit criteria are strictest for inspections.

---

## Trap 6: Thinking All Review Types Collect Metrics

**Trap:** Only formal reviews (especially inspections) collect and analyze metrics.

**Example question:**
> Which review type is MOST likely to collect metrics such as defect density and preparation time per reviewer?
>
> A. Informal Review
> B. Walkthrough
> C. Technical Review
> D. Inspection

**Wrong answer:** C — technical reviews may collect some metrics, but inspections are the standard for metrics.

**Why it's wrong:** Inspections are specifically designed to collect metrics for process improvement.

**Correct answer:** D — Inspection.

**Defense strategy:** Metrics collection is proportional to formality. Informal/walkthrough = minimal metrics. Inspection = comprehensive metrics.

---

## Trap 7: Forgetting that Reviews Can Be Used for Any Work Product

**Trap:** Reviews apply to more than just code — requirements, designs, test plans, user manuals can all be reviewed.

**Example question:**
> A team wants to find defects in their test cases before execution. Which static testing technique is MOST appropriate?
>
> A. Static Analysis
> B. Review
> C. Dynamic Testing
> D. Penetration Testing

**Wrong answer:** A — "Static Analysis" — static analysis tools analyze code, not test cases.

**Why it's wrong:** Static analysis is automated analysis of code. Test cases are reviewed by humans.

**Correct answer:** B — Review.

**Defense strategy:** Reviews can be applied to ANY work product: requirements, designs, code, test plans, user manuals, contracts.

---

## Trap 8: Confusing the Number of Reviewers

**Trap:** Too many reviewers make meetings unproductive. Too few miss defects.

**Example question:**
> For a formal inspection of a critical design document, what is the RECOMMENDED number of reviewers (excluding author, moderator, and scribe)?
>
> A. 1-2
> B. 3-7
> C. 10-15
> D. As many as possible

**Wrong answer:** D — "As many as possible" — large groups are unproductive.

**Why it's wrong:** Too many reviewers lead to chaotic discussions, scheduling problems, and diminishing returns.

**Correct answer:** B — 3-7 reviewers.

**Defense strategy:** 3-7 reviewers is the sweet spot for formal reviews. Fewer than 3 may miss defects. More than 7 becomes unwieldy.
