# 📝 Mock Exam 5 — Answer Key (Extended Practice Set)

Each answer shows the **correct option**, a **rationale**, the **Learning Objective (LO)** it tests, and the **K-level**.

---

### ✔️ Q1 — Answer: B  *(FL-1.1.1, K1)*
Testing in **reviews** (a form of static testing) **prevents defects** by detecting issues in work products early, before they are built into the software.

### ✔️ Q2 — Answer: B  *(FL-1.5.2, K1)*
In the **whole-team approach**, testers **collaborate closely** with developers and business representatives throughout, rather than working in isolation or only at the end.

### ✔️ Q3 — Answer: B  *(FL-1.3.1, K2)*
This is "**exhaustive testing is impossible**" — testing every possible combination of inputs and preconditions is infeasible except for trivial cases, so risk and priorities guide test selection.

### ✔️ Q4 — Answer: B  *(FL-1.2.3, K2)*
The causal chain is **error → defect → failure**: a human **error** introduces a **defect** in a work product, which when executed may produce a **failure**.

### ✔️ Q5 — Answer: B  *(FL-1.4.1, K2)*
**Test implementation** prepares the test environment and data and organizes test cases into runnable test procedures/suites, getting everything ready for execution.

### ✔️ Q6 — Answer: B  *(FL-1.4.5, K2)*
The **testing role** is principally responsible for **analyzing, designing, implementing, and executing** tests. Strategy, business sign-off, and project scheduling sit with other roles.

### ✔️ Q7 — Answer: B  *(FL-1.2.1, K2)*
Testing reduces the **risk of failures occurring during operation** by finding defects before release and providing information about quality.

### ✔️ Q8 — Answer: B  *(FL-1.1.2, K2)*
**Testing** finds failures (evidence that defects exist); **debugging** is the development activity that locates and fixes the defect. They are distinct and not interchangeable.

### ✔️ Q9 — Answer: B  *(FL-2.1.2, K2)*
A good practice for all lifecycles is that **test activities align with and begin during the corresponding development activity** (e.g., test analysis starts as requirements are written).

### ✔️ Q10 — Answer: B  *(FL-2.1.3, K1)*
**ATDD** (Acceptance Test-Driven Development) creates acceptance tests **collaboratively before development**, derived from acceptance criteria.

### ✔️ Q11 — Answer: B  *(FL-2.1.1, K2)*
In **sequential** models, test **levels are aligned to development phases** and execution tends to occur later (after the corresponding build phase). Continuous deployment is characteristic of DevOps/Agile.

### ✔️ Q12 — Answer: B  *(FL-2.2.3, K2)*
**Confirmation testing** re-runs the previously failing test after a fix to verify the defect is resolved. Detecting side effects of changes is *regression* testing.

### ✔️ Q13 — Answer: B  *(FL-2.2.2, K2)*
Usability is a **quality characteristic**, so usability testing is **non-functional testing**.

### ✔️ Q14 — Answer: B  *(FL-2.2.1, K2)*
**Acceptance testing** establishes confidence in the system and **validates it against business needs** and readiness for use, rather than checking internal structure or single units.

### ✔️ Q15 — Answer: B  *(FL-3.1.1, K1)*
Static testing examines **non-executing** work products. The **dynamically executing system under load** is tested by dynamic (performance) testing, not static testing.

### ✔️ Q16 — Answer: B  *(FL-3.2.5, K1)*
Success factors include **appropriately sized, focused reviews with trained participants and management support**. Oversized sessions, skipped preparation, and blaming the author all undermine reviews.

### ✔️ Q17 — Answer: B  *(FL-3.2.4, K2)*
An **informal review** is the least formal type — often with no documented process, no formal roles, and minimal or no metrics.

### ✔️ Q18 — Answer: B  *(FL-3.1.3, K2)*
A key advantage of **static testing** is finding defects **earlier** and in **non-executable** work products (e.g., requirements), before any code can be run.

### ✔️ Q19 — Answer: B  *(FL-4.1.1, K2)*
**Equivalence partitioning and boundary value analysis** are **black-box** techniques: they derive tests from the specification without reference to internal code structure.

### ✔️ Q20 — Answer: B  *(FL-4.3.3, K2)*
**Statement and branch coverage** are structural measures used in **white-box testing** to quantify how much of the code structure has been exercised.

### ✔️ Q21 — Answer: B  *(FL-4.4.2, K2)*
**Exploratory testing** shines when specifications are **missing or poor**, time is limited, or you want to **complement scripted testing** by learning the system quickly.

### ✔️ Q22 — Answer: B  *(FL-4.4.3, K2)*
Using a **usability checklist** to guide a review is **checklist-based (experience-based) testing**.

### ✔️ Q23 — Answer: B  *(FL-4.5.1, K2)*
The "**Card**" is a short written description of the story — a placeholder that prompts the **conversation** and is fleshed out by the **confirmation** (acceptance criteria).

### ✔️ Q24 — Answer: B  *(FL-4.5.2, K2)*
Good acceptance criteria are **testable, clear, and unambiguous**, so everyone agrees on when the story is done and they can be turned into acceptance tests.

### ✔️ Q25 — Answer: D  *(FL-4.2.1, K3)*
The discount partition is 10–50. Values 20, 35, and 50 fall **inside** it, while **5 is below 10** and so lies in a different (no-discount) partition.

### ✔️ Q26 — Answer: A  *(FL-4.2.2, K3)*
**Three-value BVA** at the lower boundary (10) tests the boundary and both neighbors: **9, 10, 11**.

### ✔️ Q27 — Answer: B  *(FL-4.2.3, K3)*
When two rules yield identical actions and differ only in a condition that does not affect the outcome, they can be **merged into one rule** with that condition marked "**don't care**" (—), simplifying the table.

### ✔️ Q28 — Answer: B  *(FL-4.2.4, K3)*
**0-switch coverage** (all valid transitions) requires exercising **every valid single transition at least once**. Covering pairs of consecutive transitions is **1-switch** coverage.

### ✔️ Q29 — Answer: B  *(FL-4.3.1 / FL-4.3.2, K3)*
With an **explicit ELSE**, the two branches contain different statements, so executing all statements needs both branches — **2 test cases** for statement coverage, which also satisfies branch coverage here.

### ✔️ Q30 — Answer: B  *(FL-5.1.6, K1)*
The **base** of the test pyramid is the largest layer: many **unit/component tests** (fast, cheap, stable), with fewer tests toward the top.

### ✔️ Q31 — Answer: B  *(FL-5.4.1, K2)*
**Configuration management** identifies, versions, and tracks test objects and testware so tests are **reproducible** and results are traceable to specific versions.

### ✔️ Q32 — Answer: B  *(FL-5.1.1, K2)*
A test plan contains scope, objectives, schedule, resources, and entry/exit criteria — **not** the full future source code of unbuilt features.

### ✔️ Q33 — Answer: B  *(FL-5.2.4, K2)*
**Lower-risk areas** receive **lighter, less exhaustive testing**, so effort can be concentrated on higher-risk areas where failures would be more damaging.

### ✔️ Q34 — Answer: B  *(FL-5.2.2, K2)*
A **product risk** is a risk that the product may fail to meet needs — e.g., **data corruption under concurrent use**. Staff turnover, environment delays, and budget overruns are project risks.

### ✔️ Q35 — Answer: B  *(FL-5.3.2, K2)*
**Progress reports** are produced **during** testing to show ongoing status; **completion reports** summarize outcomes at the **end** of a test activity. 

### ✔️ Q36 — Answer: B  *(FL-5.1.5, K3)*
Run the highest-priority (highest-risk) tests first: **P1 (critical), P2 (high), P3 (medium), P4 (low)** → P1, P2, P3, P4. A random order does not target risk.

### ✔️ Q37 — Answer: B  *(FL-5.2.1, K3)*
Risk level = likelihood × impact = 5 × 1 = **5**. (Note: although the numeric level is modest, a very high likelihood may still warrant attention.)

### ✔️ Q38 — Answer: B  *(FL-5.5.1, K3)*
**Severity** (technical impact) and **priority** (urgency to fix) are the key fields that drive **fix prioritization**. The other options are irrelevant to the defect's importance.

### ✔️ Q39 — Answer: B  *(FL-6.1.1, K1)*
A tool that **generates load** to evaluate performance/behavior under stress is a **performance testing tool**.

### ✔️ Q40 — Answer: B  *(FL-6.2.1, K2)*
Automation is best for **repetitive, stable, high-value checks** (e.g., regression); **exploratory and usability** testing typically remain manual. Automation still needs maintenance and does not guarantee complete coverage.
