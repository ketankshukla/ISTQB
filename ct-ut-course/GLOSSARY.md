# CT-UT Glossary

Key terms and definitions for the CT-UT exam, organized by chapter relevance.

---

## Usability Fundamentals

**Usability**
The extent to which a product can be used by specified users to achieve specified goals with effectiveness, efficiency, and satisfaction in a specified context of use (ISO 9241-11).

**User Experience (UX)**
A person's perceptions and responses resulting from the use and/or anticipated use of a product, system, or service. Broader than usability, including emotions, perceptions, and attitudes.

**Effectiveness**
The accuracy and completeness with which users achieve specified goals.

**Efficiency**
The resources expended in relation to the accuracy and completeness with which users achieve goals. Typically measured as time on task or number of steps.

**Satisfaction**
The freedom from discomfort, and positive attitudes towards the use of the product.

**Learnability**
The degree to which a product or system can be used by specified users to achieve specified goals of learning to use the product with effectiveness, efficiency, freedom from risk, and satisfaction.

**Memorability**
The degree to which a product or system can be used by specified users to achieve specified goals of remembering how to use the product.

**Error Tolerance**
The degree to which a product or system can be used by specified users to achieve specified goals with tolerable error rates.

**Cognitive Load**
The amount of mental effort required to use a system. Lower cognitive load = better usability.

**Mental Model**
The user's internal representation of how a system works. Good UX aligns with users' mental models.

**Affordance**
A property of an object that suggests how it can be used. For example, a button affords clicking.

**Signifier**
Any perceivable cue that indicates what actions are possible and how they should be performed.

## Usability Testing

**Formative Usability Testing**
Testing conducted during design and development to identify and fix usability problems early. Goal: improve the product.

**Summative Usability Testing**
Testing conducted on the final product to evaluate it against criteria. Goal: measure usability.

**Think-Aloud Protocol**
A user testing method where participants verbalize their thoughts, feelings, and opinions while performing tasks.

**Co-Discovery**
A user testing method where two participants work together on tasks and discuss their thought process.

**Retrospective Testing**
A user testing method where participants review a recording of their session and comment on their actions.

**Moderated Testing**
Usability testing where a facilitator guides the participant, asks follow-up questions, and probes for understanding.

**Unmoderated Testing**
Usability testing where participants complete tasks on their own without a facilitator. Typically done remotely.

**SUS (System Usability Scale)**
A 10-item questionnaire measuring perceived usability. Scored 0-100. Above 68 is considered above average.

**NPS (Net Promoter Score)**
A single-question loyalty metric: "How likely are you to recommend..." on a 0-10 scale. Score = % Promoters - % Detractors.

**Scenario**
A realistic description of a task the participant should perform during a usability test.

**Task**
A specific activity assigned to a participant during usability testing.

**Severity Rating**
A classification of the impact of a usability problem (e.g., cosmetic, minor, major, critical).

**Context of Use**
The users, tasks, equipment, and physical and social environments in which a product is used.

## Evaluation Methods

**Heuristic Evaluation**
An expert review method where evaluators assess an interface against established usability principles (heuristics).

**Cognitive Walkthrough**
An expert review method that simulates a user's problem-solving process at each step to evaluate learnability.

**Guideline Review**
An expert review method that checks a product against a set of documented guidelines or standards.

**Pluralistic Walkthrough**
A review method involving users, developers, and usability specialists working together.

**Persona**
A fictional character representing a user type, used to guide design and testing decisions.

**User Journey Map**
A visualization of the steps a user takes to accomplish a goal, including touchpoints and emotions.

**Eye Tracking**
A technique that records where users look and how their gaze moves across a screen.

**A/B Testing**
Comparing two versions of an interface by randomly assigning users to each and measuring outcomes.

**Card Sorting**
A technique where users organize topics into categories to help design information architecture.

**Tree Testing**
A technique to evaluate the findability of topics in a website or app's information structure.

## Accessibility

**Accessibility**
The degree to which a product or system can be used by people with the widest range of characteristics and capabilities.

**WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines)**
International guidelines for making web content accessible. Current version: 2.1.

**WCAG POUR Principles**
- **Perceivable:** Information must be presentable in ways users can perceive
- **Operable:** Interface components must be operable by all users
- **Understandable:** Information and operation must be understandable
- **Robust:** Content must work with current and future assistive technologies

**Assistive Technology**
Software or hardware that helps people with disabilities interact with digital products. Examples: screen readers, screen magnifiers, voice recognition.

**Screen Reader**
Software that reads on-screen text aloud. Examples: JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver.

**Screen Magnifier**
Software that enlarges portions of the screen for users with low vision.

**Switch Device**
An input device operated by physical actions other than a keyboard or mouse, used by people with motor disabilities.

**Alt Text**
Alternative text descriptions for images, read by screen readers.

**Keyboard Trap**
A situation where keyboard users cannot move focus away from an element.

**Color Contrast Ratio**
The difference in luminance between foreground and background colors. WCAG requires 4.5:1 for normal text.

**Inclusive Design**
Designing products that are accessible to and usable by as many people as possible, regardless of ability or circumstance.

**Universal Design**
The design of products and environments to be usable by all people, to the greatest extent possible, without adaptation.

## Disabilities

**Visual Disability**
Includes blindness, low vision, color blindness, and sensitivity to glare.

**Auditory Disability**
Includes deafness, hard of hearing, and difficulty distinguishing sounds.

**Motor Disability**
Includes paralysis, tremors, limb differences, and conditions affecting coordination.

**Cognitive Disability**
Includes dyslexia, autism, ADHD, memory impairments, and learning disabilities.

**Speech Disability**
Includes inability to speak, speech that is difficult to understand, and stuttering.

## Organizational Terms

**UX Researcher**
A professional who studies users, their needs, and behaviors to inform design decisions.

**Usability Engineer**
A professional who applies usability principles and methods to design and evaluate products.

**Shift-Left Testing**
Moving testing activities earlier in the development lifecycle. For usability, this means testing during design, not just after implementation.

**UX Maturity**
The level of user-centered practices within an organization, ranging from absent to integrated.

**Business Case**
A justification for a project or initiative that compares costs and benefits.
