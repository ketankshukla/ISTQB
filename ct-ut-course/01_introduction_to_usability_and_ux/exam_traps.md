# Chapter 1 — Introduction to Usability and UX: Exam Traps

## Trap 1: Confusing Usability and User Experience

**Trap:** Usability is a subset of UX. UX is broader, including emotions, brand perception, and the entire journey.

**Example question:**
> A user successfully completes a task quickly but feels the interface is ugly and untrustworthy. Which statement is TRUE?
>
> A. The product has good usability but poor UX
> B. The product has poor usability and good UX
> C. The product has both good usability and good UX
> D. Usability and UX are the same, so the product has good both

**Correct answer:** A — Successful and quick = good usability. Ugly and untrustworthy = poor UX (which includes aesthetics and credibility).

## Trap 2: Forgetting the "Specified Context of Use"

**Trap:** ISO 9241-11 states usability is always in a "specified context of use." It is not absolute.

**Example question:**
> A children's educational app is evaluated using adult office workers as test participants. The results show poor usability. The MOST likely problem is:
>
> A. The app is poorly designed
> B. The test participants do not match the specified context of use (children)
> C. The adults are not intelligent enough
> D. All apps have poor usability

**Correct answer:** B — Testing with the wrong users violates the "specified users" requirement of ISO 9241-11.

---

## Trap 3: Thinking Accessibility Is Separate from Usability

**Trap:** Accessibility is a sub-characteristic of usability in ISO 25010, not a separate concern.

**Example question:**
> According to ISO 25010, accessibility is:
>
> A. A completely separate quality characteristic from usability
> B. A sub-characteristic of usability
> C. Only relevant for government websites
> D. Optional for most products

**Correct answer:** B — ISO 25010 lists accessibility as a sub-characteristic of usability (along with learnability, operability, etc.).

---

## Trap 4: Confusing Affordances and Signifiers

**Trap:** An affordance is a property of an object. A signifier indicates that an action is possible. In digital design, we often add signifiers because affordances are not naturally visible.

**Example question:**
> A button on a website is styled to look three-dimensional with a shadow, suggesting it can be pressed. The shadow is:
>
> A. An affordance
> B. A signifier
> C. A constraint
> D. Feedback

**Correct answer:** B — The shadow is a signifier (a perceivable cue) that indicates the button can be pressed. The affordance (the ability to click) is inherent to a button element, but the shadow makes it visible.

---

## Trap 5: Thinking Error Messages Are Better Than Error Prevention

**Trap:** Nielsen's heuristic "error prevention" states it is better to prevent errors than to recover from them.

**Example question:**
> A system allows users to submit a form with invalid data, then shows an error message. The BEST improvement is:
>
> A. Make the error message more detailed
> B. Prevent the error by validating input in real-time and disabling submit until valid
> C. Add a help page explaining the error
> D. Use red text for the error message

**Correct answer:** B — Error prevention is more effective than error recovery.
