# 🪤 Chapter 2 — Usability Testing Process: Exam Traps

## 🪤 Trap 1: Confusing Formative and Summative Testing

**Trap:** Formative = during development, find problems. Summative = near completion, measure and validate.

**Example question:**
> A team tests a paper prototype with 5 users to identify navigation problems before development begins. This is:
>
> A. Summative usability testing
> B. Formative usability testing
> C. Acceptance testing
> D. Regression testing

**Correct answer:** B — Testing during development to find problems = formative. Low-fidelity prototype and small sample confirm this.

---

## 🪤 Trap 2: Thinking More Participants Are Always Better for Formative Testing

**Trap:** For formative testing, 5 users find ~85% of problems. Adding more has diminishing returns. Multiple rounds with 5 users each is better.

**Example question:**
> For formative usability testing, the MOST efficient approach is:
>
> A. Test with 50 participants in one round
> B. Test with 5 participants, fix issues, then test again with 5 new participants
> C. Test with 2 participants and extrapolate results
> D. Test with the entire user base

**Correct answer:** B — Multiple rounds with ~5 participants each is most efficient for formative testing.

---

## ❓ Trap 3: Leading Questions in Moderation

**Trap:** Moderators must avoid leading questions that bias participants. "Was that button hard to find?" leads the participant.

**Example question:**
> During a think-aloud session, a moderator asks: "You seemed confused by that menu. Was it poorly designed?" This is:
>
> A. Good probing
> B. A leading question that may bias the participant's response
> C. Required by the think-aloud protocol
> D. Appropriate for expert review

**Correct answer:** B — The moderator is suggesting the menu is poorly designed, biasing the participant. Better: "What are you thinking right now?"

---

## 🪤 Trap 4: Testing the User Instead of the Product

**Trap:** The fundamental principle: "We are testing the product, not you." Participants must feel safe to make mistakes.

**Example question:**
> A participant apologizes for being "bad with computers" after struggling with a task. The moderator should:
>
> A. Agree and move to the next task
> B. Reassure the participant that the difficulty reveals product problems, not personal failings
> C. End the session because the participant is not suitable
> D. Give the participant instructions on how to complete the task

**Correct answer:** B — The moderator must reinforce that the test evaluates the product, not the participant.

---

## 🪤 Trap 5: Reporting Only Problems Without Recommendations

**Trap:** Usability reports must include actionable recommendations, not just problem lists.

**Example question:**
> A usability report lists 50 problems but provides no recommendations for fixing them. The report is:
>
> A. Complete — the development team will figure out fixes
> B. Incomplete — it should include prioritized, actionable recommendations
> C. Sufficient for stakeholders
> D. Better than reports with too many suggestions

**Correct answer:** B — A usability report must include actionable recommendations. Otherwise, it is just a list of complaints without a path forward.
