# Chapter 4 — Accessibility and Inclusive Design: Exam Traps

## Trap 1: Thinking Accessibility Is Only for Blind Users

**Trap:** Accessibility covers all disabilities: visual, auditory, motor, cognitive, and speech. It is not just about screen readers.

**Example question:**
> A team tests a website with a screen reader and declares it accessible. This is:
>
> A. Sufficient — screen reader testing covers all accessibility needs
> B. Incomplete — accessibility must also consider motor, auditory, cognitive, and speech disabilities
> C. Required by WCAG
> D. The only test needed

**Correct answer:** B — Accessibility covers all disability types, not just visual impairments.

---

## Trap 2: Confusing Accessibility and Usability

**Trap:** Accessibility is a subset of usability focused on people with disabilities. They are not identical, but they overlap significantly.

**Example question:**
> A product is highly usable for the general population but completely inaccessible to screen reader users. This product is:
>
> A. Both usable and accessible
> B. Usable but not accessible
> C. Neither usable nor accessible
> D. Accessible but not usable

**Correct answer:** B — Usable for general population but inaccessible to screen reader users = usable but not accessible.

---

## Trap 3: Thinking WCAG Only Applies to Websites

**Trap:** WCAG principles apply to all digital content and interfaces, including mobile apps, desktop software, and documents.

**Example question:**
> WCAG applies to:
>
> A. Only public websites
> B. Only government websites
> C. Web content, mobile apps, and other digital interfaces
> D. Only HTML pages

**Correct answer:** C — WCAG principles apply broadly to digital content and interfaces.

---

## Trap 4: Thinking Color Contrast Is Optional

**Trap:** WCAG Level AA requires a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 for normal text. This is not optional for compliance.

**Example question:**
> A designer uses light gray text on a white background because it looks elegant. The contrast ratio is 2.5:1. For WCAG 2.1 Level AA compliance, this is:
>
> A. Acceptable because aesthetics are important
> B. A failure — Level AA requires 4.5:1 for normal text
> C. Only a problem for users with perfect vision
> D. Acceptable if the text is large

**Correct answer:** B — 2.5:1 fails WCAG 2.1 AA (requires 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text).

---

## Trap 5: Thinking Accessibility Testing Is Separate from Usability Testing

**Trap:** Accessibility testing should be integrated into the usability testing process, not treated as a separate, late-stage activity.

**Example question:**
> A team conducts all usability testing with typical users and only tests accessibility after release. The problem is:
>
> A. There is no problem — this is standard practice
> B. Accessibility issues are discovered too late and are more expensive to fix
> C. Typical users should not be tested
> D. Accessibility testing is unnecessary

**Correct answer:** B — Accessibility should be tested early and throughout, not as a late-stage add-on. Late discovery = higher fix cost.
