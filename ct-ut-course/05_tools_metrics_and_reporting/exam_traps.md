# Chapter 5 — Tools, Metrics and Reporting: Exam Traps

## Trap 1: Thinking SUS Measures Actual Usability

**Trap:** SUS measures PERCEIVED usability, not actual performance. A user may rate a system high despite poor task success.

**Example question:**
> A product has a SUS score of 85 but a task success rate of only 50%. This suggests:
>
> A. The product has excellent usability
> B. SUS measures perceived usability, which may not match actual task performance
> C. The test participants were wrong
> D. Task success rate is not important

**Correct answer:** B — SUS = perceived. Actual performance requires task metrics.

---

## Trap 2: Using Only Quantitative or Only Qualitative Data

**Trap:** Both are needed. Quantitative identifies problems; qualitative explains why.

**Example question:**
> A usability test reports that task success rate dropped from 80% to 50%. The report should also include:
>
> A. Only the statistical significance
> B. Qualitative data explaining WHY users are failing
> C. A recommendation to ignore the drop
> D. Only the SUS score

**Correct answer:** B — Quantitative identifies the problem; qualitative explains causes and guides fixes.

---

## Trap 3: Thinking NPS Is a Usability Metric

**Trap:** NPS measures loyalty/recommendation likelihood, not usability. It is broader than usability.

**Example question:**
> NPS is BEST described as a measure of:
>
> A. Task completion time
> B. Customer loyalty and likelihood to recommend
> C. System learnability
> D. Error rate

**Correct answer:** B — NPS = loyalty and recommendation likelihood, not a pure usability metric.

---

## Trap 4: Reporting Without Actionable Recommendations

**Trap:** Reports must include specific, prioritized recommendations.

**Example question:**
> A usability report lists 30 problems but no recommendations. Its value is:
>
> A. High — the development team will figure it out
> B. Limited — without recommendations, it is just a complaint list
> C. Complete — findings are enough
> D. Only for executives

**Correct answer:** B — A report without recommendations has limited practical value.

---

## Trap 5: Confusing Slips and Mistakes

**Trap:** Slips = accidental errors (know the right thing but do it wrong). Mistakes = wrong understanding (think the wrong thing is right).

**Example question:**
> A user clicks "Cancel" instead of "Submit" because the buttons are adjacent and similarly styled. This is:
>
> A. A mistake — the user misunderstood the buttons
> B. A slip — the user knew what to do but accidentally clicked the wrong button
> C. Not an error
> D. A system bug

**Correct answer:** B — Adjacent buttons with similar styling cause slips (accidental clicks).
